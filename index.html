The Thinline, the Vox, and Seven Pedals

What actually happens to your sound — pickup to speaker — and how to get warm, crunchy breakup at a volume that doesn't wake the kids.

Folk · folk-rock · alt-country · Squier Classic Vibe '60s Tele Thinline → Vox AC15C1X · June 2026

You are a folk player first. Acoustic guitar, banjo, mandolin — and when the band plugs in, a semi-hollow Squier Classic Vibe '60s Telecaster Thinline through a 15-watt Vox. That instrument and that amp already have a voice: bright, chimey, woody, a little airy. Everything on the floor in front of you is there to nudge that voice, not replace it. So before a single specification: the goal is warm, textured, crunchy breakup — at conversation volume, because there are kids asleep and neighbors close. Great tone at deafening volume isn't tone you can use. This report keeps that constraint in the foreground and stays honest about what's documented versus estimated.

How to read the confidence tags. DOC = documented in a manufacturer manual, schematic, or verified circuit trace. EST = estimated or inferred — a reasonable engineering guess where the maker publishes no number. Several boutique pedals publish almost nothing; those numbers are flagged so you stay grounded in fact.

§1 The sound you're chasing

Stated plainly, and held loosely — these are targets, not rules. When you reach for this sound, here's what it means in fact:

  • Crunch / breakup / saturation / warmth / texture — low-to-medium gain. Think Neil Young's ragged edge and Son Volt's amp-on-the-verge crunch, not high-gain anything.
  • At conversation volume. This is the hard engineering constraint. It pushes every decision toward preamp distortion and pedal saturation, and away from cranked power tubes — see §8.
  • The Thinline stays bright and airy. Two vintage-output single-coils in a semi-hollow body — chimey, woody, never hot; the job of the chain is to thicken and warm it without burying its jangle (and without tipping the f-hole into feedback).
The whole signal path is a chain of EQ and gain stages: Thinline pickups → (Comp) → (Boost/OD) → Vox preamp → tone stack → power amp → Alnico Blue speaker. Tone is what survives all of them. Most of this report is just making each stage visible.

§4 The Thinline — two pickups, a semi-hollow body, your first EQ stage

The guitar is not a neutral source feeding the chain. It is the first, and arguably most important, EQ. The Squier Classic Vibe '60s Telecaster Thinline is a semi-hollow Tele with a single f-hole and two vintage-output single-coils. Unlike an Esquire (one pickup, fixed-preset switching), this is standard Tele wiring — a 3-way blade selecting bridge / both / neck, with one master volume and one master tone. Two things shape its voice before any pedal: the pickups and the chambered body.

Specs below are from a dedicated deep-research pass (22 sources, 25 claims adversarially verified, 21 confirmed / 4 refuted). Fender's own '60s Thinline page returned 403 to direct fetch, so primary specs were cross-checked across findmyguitar, Guitar World, Sweetwater and Reverb; DCR and mod data come from owner/teardown forums (TDPRI, Squier-Talk) — the right source class, since Fender publishes neither.

Four facts matter most for how this guitar behaves in a real setup:

  • You have three distinct voices, not one. Bridge = bright Tele twang (est. peak ~3.5 kHz) that cuts through. Neck = warm, round, fatter (est. ~2.3 kHz), great for chord beds and lyrical leads. Both-in-parallel = the scooped, quacky, woody jangle — and the semi-hollow body exaggerates its airy, open character. An Esquire has none of this middle/neck range.
  • The pickups are vintage-output alnico — and DCR doesn't tell you the output. These read in the ~6–9 kΩ range depending on run, but that resistance is not a loudness spec: an Alnico-3 winding measures about the same as an Alnico-5 one yet pushes less and scoops the mids, while Alnico-5 is brighter and more balanced. Either way they favor clarity and low-to-medium gain — exactly your target. Let the dirt come from pedals and the Vox preamp, not from a hot pickup.
  • The body warms and opens the sound. The chamber + f-hole add documented acoustic resonance and "breathiness," trading a little of the solid-Tele brightness for a rounder, airier tone (some reviewers even report more sustain, not less). This is a gift for folk and alt-country — your single-coils start out less brittle than a solid Tele's.
  • Stock wiring has no treble-bleed. Roll the volume down and you lose treble as source impedance rises into cable capacitance — brightest only near full volume. To darken without losing level, roll the tone knob instead (or add the standard 0.001 µF + 100 kΩ parallel treble-bleed — a common owner mod, not stock).
In a folk-rock setup: the both-pickups middle position is your jangly strummed-rhythm voice — woody and open, perfect for folk-rock and alt-country. The neck pickup with tone backed slightly is an instant warm chord bed when a passage turns shrill. The bridge is your cutting lead/rhythm bite that survives a TS mid-hump and a bright Vox. The one watch-out is the f-hole: semi-hollow guitars can feed back sooner than a solid body at high gain or volume (a qualitative caveat — the exact threshold through your AC15 isn't measured). Keeping gain low-to-medium matches your goal anyway; mind your distance from the amp and use the volume knob to tame any howl.
If you ever mod it (per owner consensus): the most-recommended, cheap improvements are compensated brass saddles (better intonation + a little more sustain/zing from the 3-saddle bridge), a Twisted Tele neck pickup if you want a richer neck voice, a graphite nut/string tree and locking tuners for tuning stability, and a 4-way switch to add a series (fatter, louder) middle option. Note: claims that the stock bridge pickup "sounds weak" or that Tonerider/Fender-Original-Vintage swaps are the common path were refuted in research — the stock pickups are well-regarded; mod for a voice you want, not to fix a flaw.

§5 The Vox AC15C1X — preamp volume vs power-amp volume

This is the crux of the whole report, because it's where your volume constraint is won or lost. The AC15 has two volume controls that distort in completely different ways and at completely different room levels.

Where each volume sits

Master Volume

Tone Cut

The signal chain inside the amp:

Input → 12AX7 preamp → Channel Volume → [Top Boost: extra 12AX7 + cathode follower → Treble/Bass stack] → Phase Inverter → Tone Cut → Master Volume → EL84 power tubes → output transformer → Alnico Blue
  • Channel Volume up + Master down = preamp distortion at low room volume. This is the post-phase-inverter master's entire reason to exist, and it's your single most important control for the kids-asleep constraint. Bite, sizzle, attack — at bedroom SPL.
  • Master up = EL84 power-tube breakup. Warmth, sustain, compression, "bloom." It's the magic AC15 sound — and it needs real volume, because power-amp distortion is the amp making its rated wattage. The Alnico Blue breaks up a touch earlier than a Greenback, which helps, but physics has the final say. Note: the AC15C1X combo has no built-in power attenuator — the Master Volume is your only level control. (The reactive attenuator that drops 15 W → 1.5 W → 0.15 W is on the AC15CH head, not this combo. An outboard attenuator or a load box is the add-on path.)

The Top Boost EQ — and where any EQ in the chain lives

The Top Boost channel adds an extra 12AX7 (one half = gain, one half = a cathode follower with ~612 Ω output impedance driving the tone stack). Treble and Bass are interactive shelving controls over an inherently mid-scooped stack — that ~480 Hz scoop is the "Vox chime." Treble can add over 30 dB at 10 kHz. With both controls down, the stack is roughly flat (plus the scoop). So your EQ points in the whole rig are: the guitar tone knob (a low-pass), any pedal tone/EQ, the Top Boost Treble/Bass stack, and the global Tone Cut low-pass on the power-amp side. That's it — four places to shape frequency, and they cascade.

Practical Vox setting for low-volume crunch: Top Boost channel, Channel Volume up to ~2–3 o'clock for preamp grind, Master low enough for the room, Bass moderate to refill the Thinline/TS lows, Treble to taste, and use Tone Cut to shave the fizz that preamp clipping at low power-amp volume tends to leave on top. Tone Cut is your fizz tamer precisely because it acts after the preamp, on the power-amp side.

§7 Distortion is the shape of the bend

"Gain," "drive," "saturation," "fuzz" all describe one thing physically: how much a stage bends its input-vs-output transfer curve. A straight line is clean. A gently curved line is warm soft-clipping. A line that slams flat is hard clipping and fuzz.

Why this matters for warmth: the even-order harmonics that read as "warm" come from asymmetric clipping — the Green Screamer's mixed diodes and the EL84 power stage both have it. The TS9's symmetric clipping is smoother but more "mid-cocked" and odd-harmonic. For folk-rock warmth at low volume, an asymmetric soft-clipper (Green Screamer, or the Vox preamp itself) gets you closer than a textbook symmetric TS.

§8 Crunch at conversation volume — the whole point

Here is the constraint made quantitative. Two different distortion mechanisms, plotted against room volume. Preamp distortion is available the moment you turn the channel volume up — independent of how loud the room is. Power-amp bloom only arrives when the EL84s are genuinely driven, which means genuinely loud.

The low-volume crunch recipe, distilled:
  1. Get the grit before the power amp: Vox preamp (Channel Vol up) and/or a soft-clip pedal (Green Screamer, or Colour Box pushed).
  2. Keep the Master low — you're not asking the EL84s to break up.
  3. Use Tone Cut to remove the fizz that low-volume preamp clipping leaves on top.
  4. Refill low end (Green Screamer Deep, Colour Box bass shelf, or Vox Bass) — both the single-coils and any TS thin it out.
  5. A touch of compression (Keeley, blended) adds the sustain you'd otherwise only get from a cranked power amp — without the volume.

§9 The seven pedals, in technical detail

JHS Colour Box V2 — the Swiss-army preamp

A pedal modeled on a Neve console mic-preamp channel strip, and the most capable EQ and cleanest boost in your collection. Two cascaded gain stages with a STEP control setting stage gain in documented increments — +18 / +23 / +28 / +33 / +39 dB DOC — and PRE-VOL/MASTER on top, so practical gain runs from a whisper to full overdrive. The EQ is a "modified Baxandall," ±17 dB per band, with V2's new SHIFT knobs sweeping each band's center: Treble around 10 kHz (sweep ~2–30 kHz), Mid around 1 kHz (~150 Hz–2.4 kHz), Bass around 120 Hz (~20–440 Hz). A separate Hi-Pass sweeps 60–800 Hz at 6 dB/oct. A HI/LO toggle picks clean headroom vs. higher-gain grit.

Driving it — the Colour Box as color and overdrive, not just EQ

This is the part most people miss: the Colour Box is fundamentally a preamp that distorts, and its distortion is a different animal from a Tube Screamer's. Three things create its "color":

  • STEP + PRE-VOL = where the dirt comes from. STEP sets how hot each gain stage runs (+18 → +39 dB); PRE-VOL sits between the two stages and decides how hard the first stage slams the second. Crank PRE-VOL and you walk smoothly from clean → grit → thick overdrive → fuzzy preamp breakup. MASTER then sets output independently, so you can drive it hard and still come out at unity (or boost). High STEP + high PRE-VOL + low MASTER = saturated, controlled level — the classic "drive the preamp, tame the output" move.
  • HI/LO toggle picks the gain structure. LO = clean headroom (your transparent-boost mode). HI = higher-gain, earlier-breakup mode — flip it when you want the box itself to be the overdrive.
  • The transformer is the "Neve" in the color. V2 adds an output transformer; transformer saturation plus the Class-A-style gain stages give a fuller-range, harmonically rich, gently compressed grit — broad and hi-fi, with real low end intact, the opposite of the TS's narrow mid-hump. See the teal curve in §7: a softer, fuller bend you can then shape with the ±17 dB EQ — you sculpt the distortion's voice, which a TS will never let you do.
Three ways to use it for color/drive:
  1. Always-on Neve "glue": STEP 2–3, PRE-VOL ~10–11 o'clock, HI/LO=LO, MASTER at unity, EQ near flat. Subtle harmonic thickening + transformer warmth on everything — fattens the Thinline's clean single-coils without obvious dirt.
  2. Amp-in-a-box overdrive: HI mode, STEP 4–5, PRE-VOL high, MASTER for level, then EQ to taste. A full overdrive that distorts on its own — you can run it into the Vox's clean Normal channel (or even straight to a power amp) and never touch the Vox master. This is a low-volume crunch path that bypasses power-tube volume entirely.
  3. Neve-flavored stacked boost: moderate STEP + PRE-VOL into the Vox Top Boost preamp — pushes the amp into grind with a fuller, EQ-shaped front end instead of a TS mid-hump. Good when you want crunch and kept low end.
For you: this one pedal is your transparent clean boost (EQ flat, HI/LO=LO, +39 dB on tap), your tone-shaping Swiss army knife (carve harshness, add body and air to the Thinline), and a Neve-voiced overdrive / amp-in-a-box that gives you crunch at any volume without the Vox power amp. It is the most flexible piece on your board.

Ibanez TS9 — the reference mid-hump

The archetype. JRC4558D op-amp, two 1N4148 diodes in anti-parallel in the feedback loop = soft symmetric clipping. Drive-stage gain runs +21.5 dB (min) to +41.5 dB (max) DOC, but that gain is heavily frequency-shaped: a high-pass around 720 Hz in the clipping stage cuts bass, and the active tone section adds a treble shelf. The result is the famous mid-hump centered ~720 Hz–1 kHz — and it's structural: turn the drive to zero and the hump and bass-cut are still there. Input ~500 kΩ, buffered bypass, the TS9's 470 Ω/100 k output network makes it slightly brighter than a TS808.

BBE Green Screamer V2 — the more-transparent TS

A TS-derivative with real improvements for your use. 4558 op-amp; V1's reverse-engineered clipping used mixed diodes (1N4001 + 1N914) — asymmetric, a touch more even-harmonic "amp" character than the TS's symmetric pair. Documented: 1 MΩ input (less treble-loading of the single-coils than the TS's 500 k), 1 kΩ output, and a 3-position Deep switch — 0 flat / 1 mid-upper-bass / 2 bass DOC, your one knob for refilling the low end a TS throws away. BBE markets "greater transparency" but their own copy admits it still "emphasizes midrange and pulls out some low end" — so it's the most transparent TS here, not a clean boost. No frequency or dB curves are published; treat its voicing numbers as EST.

EHX Analogizer — analog color, not boost

Not a clean boost and not (quite) a delay — a short bucket-brigade analog delay (Spread = 3.5–65 ms DOC) summed with your dry signal, plus up to +26 dB of gain. At short Spread it comb-filters and thickens; longer becomes slapback. The "analog warmth" is the BBD's intrinsic treble roll-off — there's no tone knob, the high-cut is structural (~3–6 kHz EST). Controls are Volume, Blend, Gain, Spread (not "Color/Tone/Blend"). Useful as a thickener/doubler on the Thinline's bright single-coils, or a subtle high-cut to take edge off — but it always colors.

Keeley Compressor Plus — sustain + a real tone knob

An OTA (Ross-circuit lineage) compressor: Sustain, Level, Blend, Tone, plus a Single-Coil/Humbucker toggle that's actually a 2-state release control. The Blend knob enables parallel "New York" compression — squeeze underneath while the dry transients and highs pass through, which keeps the Thinline's attack intact. The Tone knob is a genuine treble-emphasis circuit (6.8 nF cap + ~10 k pot, ~2 kHz pivot EST) applied only to the compressed path — it adds back the highs compression naturally dulls. Level provides makeup gain / boost (dB unpublished). For folk fingerpicking and even strums on the Thinline, this is your sustain-and-evenness tool — and, blended, it fakes some of the power-amp's compression at low volume.

TC Electronic HyperGravity — studio multiband squeeze

A digital compressor running the MD3 multiband algorithm from the TC System 6000. Three modes via toggle: Spectra (multiband — compresses low/mid/high independently, so bass transients don't pump the highs down → more transparent, keeps brightness), Vintage (modeled single-band stompbox squeeze), and TonePrint (load/author custom). Analog dry-through (zero added latency on the dry path), Blend for parallel compression, documented 1 MΩ in / 100 Ω out DOC. The multiband Spectra mode is the more "invisible" compressor; the Vintage mode is the more obvious, country-snap one. Internal ms/ratio/crossover numbers are proprietary EST.

Beautiful Noise Exploder — the loud one

A boutique hard-clipping distortion/fuzz distilled from the maker's shoegaze flagship — not a clean boost. ~1000× pre-clip gain (≈+60 dB EST), a Sym/Off/Asym clipping switch (Off = op-amp self-overload, the loosest/closest-to-overdrive voice), a 3-band active EQ, and a blendable white-noise generator with its own envelope and tone. Buffered bypass, 9 V. This is your texture/noise/ wall-of-sound color for folktronica and experimental work — used sparingly, the "Off" mode with EQ at noon is its most amp-like, but it lives at the distortion end of the map, not the clean-boost end.

§10 Chains for your genres — grounded starting points

Targets, not rules. Each is a place to start, then trust your ears. Signal order assumes comp early, dirt next, into the Vox Top Boost channel.

Folk-rock crunch at low volume (the main goal)

Thinline bridge (or both), tone ~8 → Keeley (light, Blend ~10 o'clock) → Green Screamer (Gain ~10 o'clock, Deep=1, Tone noon) → Vox Top Boost: Ch.Vol 2 o'clock, Master low, Bass ~1 o'clock, Treble ~11, Tone Cut ~10 o'clock

Asymmetric soft-clip warmth from the Green Screamer + Vox preamp grind, lows refilled by Deep, fizz shaved by Tone Cut, sustain faked by the blended compressor. Crunch without the SPL. Watch the f-hole feedback if you creep the gain or volume up.

Alt-country jangle & bite

Thinline bridge (bright) → Keeley (Tone ~1 o'clock to restore highs, Blend 12) → TC HyperGravity Vintage for snap, OR Green Screamer just breaking up → Vox: Treble ~1 o'clock, Bass ~11, Tone Cut low

Let the bridge pickup's resonant peak and the Vox chime do the cutting; compressor adds the country squeeze and sustain. The both-pickups position gives a janglier, woodier alternate.

Clean, loud-when-needed lead boost (transparent)

Thinline (any position) → Colour Box V2 (EQ flat, HI/LO=LO, STEP 2–3, Master for +6–12 dB) → Vox

Pure level for solos with zero tone change — the one genuinely transparent boost. Push STEP higher and it becomes a Neve-flavored overdrive instead.

Folktronica texture / experimental

Thinline → Exploder (Off mode, EQ to taste, W/N low) or Analogizer (Spread ~9–11 o'clock to thicken) → Colour Box for EQ sculpting → Vox clean-ish

Color and noise as instruments. Colour Box afterwards tames or shapes whatever the texture pedals throw out.

Warm chord bed when a passage gets shrill

Thinline neck pickup, tone ~6 → Green Screamer low gain → Vox Bass up, Tone Cut up

The neck pickup's lower resonant peak plus a rolled tone and Tone Cut gives an instant warm, round, woody voice — the semi-hollow body adds the air.
