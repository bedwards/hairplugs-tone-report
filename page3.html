The idea: a four-stage escalator

Every rig here follows one template: two analog “Gain+EQ” pedals — each with a capable, opinionated multi-band EQ (bass/mid/treble that boost and cut, not a single tone knob) plus a gain control and a little of its own warmth — and two colored dirt pedals of deliberately different flavors, the last of which reaches full explosion.

Two drivers, two clippers

The chain pairs into two cells: Gain+EQ → dirt, twice. Each Gain+EQ box stays mostly clean and uses its output to drive the clipper after it — and because you shape the tone with an active EQ before the clip, you choose how each dirt pedal distorts (push mids for grind, cut lows to tighten, roll highs to tame a bright amp). The clip bakes that choice into its harmonics. These boxes are never sterile: a console/transformer preamp like the Colour Box adds gentle even-harmonic warmth even at its cleanest, seasoning the whole chain.

Clipping symmetry — why the order of the dirts matters

Symmetric clipping (both halves of the wave clip equally) makes mostly odd harmonics — tight, articulate, classic. Asymmetric clipping adds the even (2nd) harmonic — warmer, fuller, more tube-like. Put symmetric dirt earlier as a tight foundation and an asymmetric voice last, and the biggest, most-saturated wall comes out warm and full instead of just buzzy. (The Heart rig nails this — its terminal Exploder runs on its Asym setting. Among buyable terminals the trade is real: a Deluxe Big Muff brings the taming tools but clips symmetric; a Walrus Eras keeps the asymmetric warmth at the cost of some wall.)

The bypass switches are the instrument

Four footswitches, stomped cumulatively, walk up a ladder of both level and distortion: clean → subtle color → first dirt → explosion. The clean rungs are deliberately small steps (the ear hears tiny changes easily down there); the dirty rungs are big jumps (saturation hides nuance up high, and you can tweak knobs). One knob-setup, played with the feet — verse to climax without ever stopping.

The goal

A foot-controlled range from bedroom-clean to wall-of-sound, voiced for a bright Telecaster into a chimey Vox, where every rung is a usable, intentional sound. The two active-EQ drivers are what make the dirt sound chosen rather than generic — and what keep a bright rig from turning brittle as the gain climbs.
